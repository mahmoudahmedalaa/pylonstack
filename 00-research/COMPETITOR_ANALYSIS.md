## Per-Competitor Deep Dive

### Competitor 1: StackShare

**What they do well:**
- Large community—companies voluntarily share their stacks
- Good discoverability (Google SEO, tech profiles)
- "Stack decisions" feature shows reasoning behind tool choices

**What they do poorly:**
- Data is often stale (company profiles from 2019 still showing)
- No recommendation engine — purely passive browsing
- No cost analysis or projections
- Clunky UI, feels circa 2018
- Pivoted to enterprise sales, neglected individual devs

**User complaints (from reviews):**
- "Great concept but the data hasn't been updated in years"
- "I can see what Netflix uses, but that doesn't help me decide for my side project"
- "No way to filter by budget or project type"

**Pricing model:**
- Free for individuals, enterprise plans for company profiles

**Key takeaway:**
- StackShare validated the market need but failed to evolve. The gap is clear: **personalized, actionable recommendations** rather than passive browsing.

---

### Competitor 2: StacksFinder

**What they do well:**
- Modern, clean UI
- Deterministic scoring algorithm across 6 criteria
- Structured comparisons across categories (Backend, Hosting, Frontend, ORMs)
- Data-driven approach with numbered scores

**What they do poorly:**
- No AI or contextual recommendations
- Doesn't consider the user's specific project constraints
- No stack builder — can only compare individual tools
- No cost projections or phased roadmaps
- Limited tool coverage (focused on web frameworks)

**Pricing model:**
- Free (seems early-stage / indie project)

**Key takeaway:**
- Good at structured comparisons but misses the **holistic stack assembly** use case. Proves demand for data-driven tool evaluation.

---

### Competitor 3: Bejamas (Web Tech Comparison)

**What they do well:**
- Clean comparison tool for web-specific categories
- Categories: frameworks, headless CMS, static site generators, hosting
- Agency backing gives credibility

**What they do poorly:**
- Limited to web development stack only
- Comparison tool is shallow — doesn't account for budget, scale, or team size
- No personalization or recommendations
- Primary business is agency services, comparison tool is a marketing side-feature

**Pricing model:**
- Free tool (drives leads to their agency services)

**Key takeaway:**
- Validates that devs seek structured comparisons. But treating it as a **lead-gen side project** means it will never be as deep as a dedicated product.

## Differentiation Strategy

### Where Tech Stack Engine wins:
1. **AI-powered stack assembly** — No competitor offers "tell me what you're building and I'll recommend the full stack"
2. **Phased roadmaps with cost projections** — MVP → Growth → Scale milestones with monthly burn rates
3. **Interactive drag-and-drop builder** — Visual, tactile experience for assembling and rearranging stacks
4. **Project-contextualized recommendations** — Budget, team size, timeline, and scaling needs all inform suggestions
5. **Questionnaire wizard flow** — Guided onboarding that eliminates decision fatigue step-by-step

### Where competitors win (and we accept the gap for MVP):
1. **StackShare's community data** — Years of company stack profiles; we'll build ours over time
2. **G2's review volume** — Millions of reviews; we focus on developer tools specifically
3. **Reddit's breadth** — Covers every niche; we offer structured, actionable output instead

### My unique value proposition (one sentence):
> **Tech Stack Engine is the AI-powered stack advisor that assembles your complete technology stack based on your project's specific needs, budget, and growth plan — then gives you a phased roadmap to get there.**
